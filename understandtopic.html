How To Research a
Topic

Understanding the Basics of the Topic

- Find a general summary online. Googling the topic and reading the first few links should give you a decent idea of the key components of the issue; however, keep in mind that you will have to do further, focused research to understand the nuances of the issue. 
- Identify the main actors, their interests, countries’ stances in UN assemblies, and the status quo. Look at official statements from your country and how your country voted on past treatises and legislation.
- WIKIPEDIA IS A BIBLE, NOT A SOURCE 
- Use Wikipedia to understand the gist of it, but never cite Wikipedia! Other delegates have also read that article, and they will probably recognize when you’ve copy-pasted information into your speech. 
- Read the article, then check out the resources listed at the bottom of the Wiki page. 

Collecting Relevant, Reliable Research

- Studies, UN reports, university articles, and official reports are your best friend (Command+F on Mac to search for a specific phrase). Statistics will always help you, but don’t expect to use every page of information you find. 
- News outlets put information into context well and often link to other resources. Thus, choosing an unbiased or at least credible source is key. 
- Generally, people trust sources like: NYTimes, BBC, The Wall Street Journal, The WashPost, The Economist, Politico, The New Yorker, Foreign Affairs, The New Yorker, The Associated Press, Bloomberg, and Reuters (Forbes, 2017). 
- Check the date of publishing on every article you want to use! 
- Limit the range of time for your sources! 
- Think about your stance on the issue, and Google relevant keywords to make your starting argument. 
- Understand that international issues tend to be multi-faceted. The resolution will have to address the symptoms AND causes of the issue. Your research should as well. 
- Look at past international action and figure out why it didn’t work. Often, the issues stem from the structure of the legislation or the lack of a comprehensive plan, so your resolution will address the problems past international actions failed to fix. 
- Avoid getting caught up in the details or any one keyword. Just as important as finding evidence is understanding the logic behind it.  

Organizing Your Evidence

- Cutting cards is a method used to organize evidence and helps users easily locate the most relevant evidence. 
- The debate guru has a more in-depth guide on cutting cards, but the basics are: 
- Tagline (summary of evidence, might say what argument it refutes or strengthens), Source (to be able to cite), and Body (your evidence). 
- The Body should not contain the whole article, just the most relevant part. 
- Underline important parts, shrink irrelevant parts, and highlight the parts you want to say in your speech. 
- To easily find evidence mid-debate, breaking it into these categories is a good start: Context, Country Stances/Interests, Your Argument/Issues, Evidence Refuting Opponents, General Info. These categories may overlap, so change as needed. Separate symptoms from causes of the issue.
